Your Rights When a Debt Collector Sues You

Understanding Debt Collection Lawsuits

A debt collection lawsuit is a civil case filed by a creditor or debt buyer to collect money you allegedly owe. The plaintiff must prove: a valid debt exists, you are the person who owes it, the amount claimed is correct, and they have the legal right to collect (standing). If any of these elements is missing, you can win.

Original Creditor vs. Debt Buyer

Original creditors (the bank or company you borrowed from) generally have strong documentation. Debt buyers (companies that purchased your debt for pennies on the dollar) often do not. They may lack the original agreement, complete payment history, or proof of ownership. This is your strongest defense against debt buyer lawsuits.

How to Respond

1. Read the complaint and note every claim. 2. File an answer before the deadline (20-30 days). 3. Deny anything you cannot verify. 4. Raise affirmative defenses. 5. Request the plaintiff prove standing and produce the original agreement. Step-by-step lawsuit defense guide.

Affirmative Defenses

Statute of limitations expired. Lack of standing (debt buyer cannot prove ownership). Improper service. Incorrect amount. Account is not yours. Prior settlement or payment. Violation of the FDCPA during collection.

Check if your debt is time-barred.

Settlement Options

Many lawsuits settle before trial. Typical settlement: 40-60% of the claimed balance. Get everything in writing before paying. Make sure the agreement says the debt is "settled in full" or "satisfied." Payment plans are often available.

The Bankruptcy Alternative

If you are facing multiple lawsuits or the total debt exceeds what you can manage, bankruptcy stops all collection lawsuits immediately and discharges the underlying debt. What happens in bankruptcy.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens if I ignore a debt collection lawsuit?

The court enters a default judgment. The creditor can then garnish wages, levy bank accounts, and place liens on property.

Can I represent myself in a debt lawsuit?

Yes. Many people successfully defend debt lawsuits pro se, especially against debt buyers who lack documentation.

How much does it cost to defend a debt lawsuit?

Filing an answer is usually free or low cost. If you cannot afford an attorney, legal aid organizations may help.
